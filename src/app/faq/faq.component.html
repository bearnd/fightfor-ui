General Questions

What is this app for?
Maybe a smidge of context first: In the past 5 years we lost 2 dear friends to bowel cancer while they were still in their 20s. In both cases, after their doctors had essentially given up on them, they asked us to try and find any information we can for cutting-edge technology or treatment options that might help them.

Naturally, we started scouring the web; clinical-trials, academic articles, emailing professors and medical professionals we knew from our time in academia, all the way down to forums, Reddit, and the such. What we realised at the time was how hard it was to distinguish between viable science, anecdotal information, and outright scams. Even when it came to legitimate clinical-trials and articles it was often hard to make sense of what each trial aimed at doing and what treatment participants would receive.

As such, we decided that it shouldn't be this hard to make sense of the treasure-trove of information that exists on the web when it comes down to an ailment and the treatment options one might have. We decided that just because some doctor says there's nothing more they can do that there's isn't someone else out there that can do more. We hoped that by putting together an app that allows a person to find all options they may have that they'd have a fighting chance. Hence FightFor was envisioned.

What FightFor does is by no means magic nor does it constitute medical advice. It brings together data from a variety of incredible resources which are, however, hard to navigate and understand. It joins the data across these resources together, cleans up the data as much as possible and allows the user to search for a disease and be presented with clinical-trials around the world in, what we hope is, a simplified manner attempting to make the aim, criteria, and procedures of a trial understandable by an everyday person. It also provides aggregate information with trials by country, institution, treatment modality, etc. We would not presume to tell you what you need to do. What we can do is help you find who you could reach out in order to get the help you may need.
Why should I use your service? Can't I just find this information on my own?
You absolutely, positively can! In fact if you look under the 'Privacy & Transparency' panel you'll see we list out all our data-sources and how you can and how you can explore, search through them, and even download copies of your own!

The reason we put this service together was because we found that, while invaluable, existing datasets and their corresponding resources are difficult to navigate and often geared towards academics or medical professionals. The data presented in those resources are teeming with complex terminology that makes it hard for the average person to make an informed decision or appeal to the right people.

In addition, as a lot of the data available in the aforementioned resources are entered manually by real people they tend to be non-canonical and difficult to disambiguate. For example, exploring said datasets you will find that the city of Zurich, Switzerland appears as 'ZÃ¼rich', 'Zurich', and 'Zuerich', making hard to find all clinical-trials performed in that location. This trend is even more apparent when attempting to find information on a clinic/hospital. For example, the renowned Mayo Clinic is a massive institution and can appear in the datasets as 'Mayo Clinic Jacksonville', 'Mayo Clinic - Jacksonville', 'Mayo Clinic in Arizona', 'Mayo Clinic Arizona', too name but a few. As such, getting any aggregate information on a location, institution, or even disease and treatment becomes incredibly cumbersome.

These are some of the problems FightFor tries to solve:
  • Bringing together data from disparate data-sources and joining them in a consistent manner.
  • Displaying the collected information in a structured and simplified manner that can be explored and understood by the average person.
  • Accounting for discrepancies in the data and 'cleaning' it by canonicalizing terminology, locations, institutions, etc.
  • Providing aggregate information with regards to diseases, treatments, facilities, etc.

Privacy & Transparency

Where does the data I see come from? Can I trust it?
We wanna be as transparent and honest as possible so here are the sources we tap into to display the data you see:

  • ClinicalTrials.gov : ClinicalTrials.gov is a fantastic resource provided by the U.S. National Library of Medicine (NLM), the world's largest medical library.

    This resource serves as a free portal where previously completed, ongoing, and even future clinical-trials around the world are registered and made available to the general public.

    Information on downloading the ClinicalTrials.gov data can be found under https://clinicaltrials .gov/ct2/resources/download while the entire dataset can be downloaded as a large collection of XML files under https://clinicaltrials.gov/AllPublicXML.zip. These files can be parsed or viewed with any standard XML parser/editor while the XSD schema can be found under https:// clinicaltrials.gov/ct2/html/images/info/public.xsd.

    We ingest the data-dump mentioned above but in order to keep our datasets up to date we depend on the RSS feed available under https://clinicaltrials.gov/ct2/results/rss.xml to learn what studies have been updated/added which we check every few minutes. For every new/updated entry, we retrieve the corresponding study in the same XML format as above by using its ID and retrieving through a URL such as https://clinicaltrials.gov/ct2/show/NCT00001372?displayxml=true.


  • PubMed/MEDLINE : PubMed is a free search-engine provided by the U.S. National Library of Medicine (NLM) which allows one to search and browse the data in the MEDLINE database of references and abstracts on life sciences and biomedical topics.

    This resource provides an up-to-date index of academic articles relating to medical topics and can be used to understand what the state-of-the-art is in terms of treatments, our understanding of diseases, and the human body in general.

    Everything you need to know to retrieve this data can be found under https://www.nlm.nih.gov/databases/download/pubmed_medline.html. The MEDLINE data can be downloaded via FTP from ftp://ftp.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/baseline where an annually released baseline collection of XML files is stored. In addition, daily updates to the aforementioned baseline are released under ftp://ftp.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/updatefiles/ . The DTD schema to which the XML files adhere can be found under https://dtd.nlm.nih.gov/ncbi/pubmed/out/pubmed_180101.dtd while https://www.nlm.nih.gov/bsd/licensee/data_elements_doc.html provides some very useful documentation with regards to the file-format.

    We ingest the baseline data once a year when released and update our data with the daily-released files as they become available. Following the recommendation of the PubMed/MEDLINE staff, our entire PubMed database is wiped and re-ingested on an annual basis to ensure we have an accurate copy of the data.


  • Medical Subject Headings (MeSH) Another wonderful resource provided by the U.S. National Library of Medicine (NLM) which defines a carefully moderated thesaurus of medical vocabulary.

    This thesaurus provides a standardized way of referring to diseases, treatments, medication, etc in order to disambiguate between their different names and ensure an understanding of what clinical-trials and academic articles refer to.

    An interactive browser and search-engine of the MeSH dataset can be found under https://meshb.nlm.nih.gov/search . The MeSH dataset can be downloaded in a variety of formats under https://www.nlm.nih.gov/databases/download/mesh.html where documentation and schemata for the different formats can be found as well.

    We ingest the XML data on an annual basis by retrieving and processing the XML files over FTP from ftp://nlmpubs.nlm.nih.gov/online/mesh/MESH_FILES/xmlmesh/ .


  • Unified Medical Language System (UMLS) : UMLS is, at its core, a meta-thesaurus, and provides definitions, synonyms, as well as links between different medical terms.

    Information on downloading this dataset can be found under https://www.nlm.nih.gov/research/umls/licensedcontent/umlsknowledgesources.html . However, at the time of writing, in order to retrieve this data one needs a UMLS account which can be requested by contacting the UMLS stuff under https://uts.nlm.nih.gov/license.html . We should mention that we found the UMLS staff to be extremely helpful so upon application you should receive your credentials within a few days. Once retrieved the dataset needs to be processed via the UMLS MetamorphoSys tool information on which can be found under https://www.nlm.nih.gov/research/umls/implementation_resources/metamorphosys/help.html.

    This dataset is released on a semi-annual basis which we retrieve, process with UMLS MetamorphoSys, and then use the resulting files to retrieve MeSH synonyms and definitions. These are used to allow a user to search via colloquial terms like 'bowel cancer' and retrieve data associated with the canonical MeSH term, e.g., 'Colorectal Carninoma'. In addition, we use the UMLS data to provide simple definitions for the various terms that appear in this app.
Why are you asking for my email address?
We truly don't want your email address! Unfortunately there's only two of us so we couldn't roll out our own functionality for things like authentication, error-reporting & feedback, and live-chat. For these features we had to depend on external services (see XXX) which typically require you, the user, to provide an email address.

The email address you provide upon signing up is used for:

  • Receiving password reset emails in case you've forgotten your password.
  • Responding to feedback you may provide us with in case you encounter an error in our application. We would typically use this to apologise for any error you might have encountered and notify you when said error is resolved.
  • Responding to a question you may raise via our live-chat widget as we may not be online when you reach out but we'd really love to get back to you and help in any way we can.
However, we encourage you to take your privacy in your own hands and not give us your 'real' email address! There are dozens of entirely free disposable services online that can create a 'fake' email address you can use with this or any other service. You can use these fake addresses to sign up on different services but since most services require you to receive and verify an email, these services also allow you to check the 'virtual' mailboxes associated with these addresses for incoming messages and perform some verification action, password reset, etc.

A few such services, which we've researched and tested ourselves, are:

  • Maildrop
  • Mailinator
  • MailSac
  • GuerrillaMail
  • TempMail
A simple Google search for 'disposable email address' or 'temporary email address' will yield dozens of services like the ones above. Just make sure to select a service where the 'fake' address doesn't expire so you will be able to use it in the future should you forget your password. We suggest that you carefully read the documentation of the service you decide to go with and make sure it's right for your purposes.

Pricing

Why isn't this service free?
Sigh, we wish we could provide this service for free but we just don't have the money. We did not want to allow for external investors to dictate how this service is operated and we feel that tracking our users and selling their data to ad-agencies would be deplorable, especially given the sensitive nature of data around a person's health and wellbeing. Thus, we're funding this service entirely out of our own savings and it isn't cheap. Our database is currently sitting at 60GBs and growing constantly and this doesn't account for DB backups, server-costs to keep the data up-to-date, additional server-costs to search through and aggregate the data, bandwidth costs, external services, etc.

In addition, we were tired of working day-in and day-out realizing someone else's dream and we wanted to do something that may actually help people. Hence, we're hoping that this service can help support us so that we can stop working for one massive corporation or another and allow us to focus on work that actually fulfils us.

There's no guarantee that FightFor will work out. People might not want to pay for data that is publicly available. They might feel that it doesn't provide enough additional value to justify the cost. People might never trust a service that deals with sensitive information regarding their health. For all we know we might be unable to offset the cost of running this service and have to get a job again. However, we believe in what this service is trying to accomplish so should we fail we promise to open-source the entirety of our code and hopefully someone out there will have the means to provide it to people for free.
